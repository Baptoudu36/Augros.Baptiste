Abstract. The DEPOXY project investigates how local muscle oxygenation dynamics, measured by near-infrared spectroscopy (mNIRS), relate to global pulmonary oxygen uptake (VO2p) during treadmill running under three commercially distinct footwear conditions. Signals from four asynchronous modalities — portable metabolic analyser (COSMED K5), mNIRS (vastus lateralis and gastrocnemius), force plates, and heart rate — are synchronised and pre-processed through a bespoke Python pipeline before statistical inference is performed in R using linear mixed models and non-parametric tests. The project is currently in a single-participant exploratory phase (N = 1) and is designed to scale to approximately 20 participants. No cohort-level results are reported here; the report focuses on methodological rigour, pipeline reproducibility, and the scientific rationale underpinning each analytical step.

1. Scientific Context and Research Question

Running economy — the energy demand for a given running velocity — is a central determinant of endurance performance and is influenced by both metabolic and mechanical factors. Recent advances in footwear technology, particularly midsole foam compounds and embedded carbon-fibre plates, have attracted significant research interest because they alter the energy return profile of each foot-strike and therefore potentially reduce the metabolic cost of locomotion.

Global oxygen uptake (VO2p, mL·min⁻¹·kg⁻¹) measured via portable metabolic analysers has long served as the gold standard for quantifying running economy. However, VO2p integrates contributions from all active muscles and organs, masking local muscle-level adaptations. Near-infrared spectroscopy (NIRS) applied directly over working musculature provides a complementary window by continuously tracking oxygenated haemoglobin (O2Hb), deoxygenated haemoglobin (HHb), tissue saturation index (TSI, %), and total haemoglobin (tHb) at the site of interest.

The central research question of the DEPOXY project is therefore: how do local muscle oxygenation variations (mNIRS) relate to global oxygen consumption (VO2p) during locomotion, and do these relationships differ across footwear conditions? The primary objective is to develop indicators of local muscle oxygen cost and evaluate their relationship with VO2p. Secondary objectives include comparing footwear-induced differences in mNIRS signals (TSI, O2Hb, HHb, tHb), characterising potential dissociations between VO2p and local oxygenation, and assessing inter-individual variability as the participant cohort grows.

2. Experimental Design

2.1 Protocol

Each experimental session follows a continuous treadmill protocol at a fixed speed of 12.4 km/h. Three footwear conditions are administered in an ABCCBA counterbalanced crossover order, ensuring that position effects and fatigue are balanced across conditions. Each footwear bout is bracketed by a baseline and a recovery phase to capture the onset and offset kinetics of each signal modality. 3.1 Data Ingestion and Numeric Conversion

Each modality is stored as an independent CSV file: COSMED K5 metabolic data (Données K5 Allan.csv), ground reaction forces (Données FP Allan.csv), muscle oxygenation from the TrainRed Fyer and Plus sensors (Données NNIRS Allan.csv), and — for the current exploratory participant only — heart rate from a personal watch export (Données FC Allan.csv), given that the COSMED K5 heart rate channel was unavailable for this individual. Raw sensor exports commonly contain header artefacts, string-encoded numeric fields, and locale-specific decimal separators. The ingestion step coerces all relevant columns to numeric types, replacing non-parseable entries with NaN tokens that are propagated through the pipeline for explicit handling. This step prevents silent type-mismatch errors that would otherwise corrupt downstream arithmetic operations. All participant identifiers are anonymised at this stage: file-level metadata containing participant names is stripped and replaced with anonymised participant codes before any analytical processing begins.

3.2 COSMED Trigger Extraction and Fallback Reconstruction

The COSMED K5 breathes an event-marker channel that records experimenter-defined triggers corresponding to the onset and offset of each experimental phase. These markers serve as the common temporal reference against which all other modalities are aligned. When trigger events are clearly present in the K5 export, they are extracted directly. A fallback reconstruction algorithm is applied when triggers are absent or corrupted: it uses the known nominal protocol durations and the breath timestamps to infer phase boundaries programmatically. This dual-path approach ensures pipeline robustness across recording quality variations.

3.3 Temporal Alignment onto a Common Time Axis

Once the COSMED trigger timestamps are established, a common time axis (in seconds from session onset) is constructed. Each modality's timestamp column is re-expressed relative to this reference, converting device-specific clock readings into a unified temporal coordinate. This step resolves the "independent clocks" problem: without it, even a 1-second drift between the K5 and the NIRS device over a 30-minute session would produce a misalignment of up to ~10 breathing cycles, severely corrupting phase-by-phase comparisons.

3.4 Interpolation of Missing Observations

Breath-by-breath VO2 data are inherently irregular in time, with inter-breath intervals varying from less than two seconds during high-intensity effort to more than five seconds at rest. Force-plate data are captured at ~1000 Hz but may contain brief dropout windows. Linear interpolation is applied to upsample breath-by-breath signals onto the common time grid and to fill short dropout gaps, preserving the signal's temporal dynamics. Gaps exceeding a modality-specific threshold are flagged rather than interpolated, so that analysts are aware of data quality limitations.

3.5 Signal Filtering

NIRS signals are susceptible to motion artefacts, particularly during running. A low-pass filter (Butterworth design, cutoff tuned to the expected physiological bandwidth of O2Hb and HHb fluctuations) attenuates high-frequency mechanical noise while preserving the slower, metabolically driven oxygenation trends of interest. Force-plate signals, sampled at high frequency, are similarly filtered to isolate strike-level biomechanical features. The filter parameters are documented in the project Python scripts to ensure reproducibility.

3.6 Phase Segmentation and Export

Using the aligned trigger timestamps, each sample across all modalities is labelled with a phase identifier (Baseline_pre, Run, Recovery, Baseline_post) and a condition identifier (A, B, or C). The result is a single longitudinal dataset in which each row represents a time sample and each column corresponds to a modality-specific signal or metadata variable. This structured export is the direct input to main.Rmd.

4.2 Rationale for Linear Mixed Models

LMMs are the appropriate framework for this design because they simultaneously accommodate the within-participant repeated-measures structure (multiple footwear conditions per participant), the planned multi-participant extension (~20 participants), and missing data arising from signal dropouts — all without listwise deletion. The random-effects structure captures the substantial inter-individual physiological variability expected in running economy research. The lmerTest package extends lme4 to provide Satterthwaite-approximated degrees of freedom and p-values, which are essential for valid inference in unbalanced designs.

5. Physiological Interpretation Framework

5.1 VO2p vs. Local Oxygenation: Complementary Signals

Global VO2p reflects the net oxidative energy demand of the whole body and is sensitive to ventilatory and cardiac limitations, not only muscular ones. In contrast, HHb measured over the vastus lateralis or gastrocnemius reflects the local balance between O2 delivery and O2 extraction at that specific site. These two signals need not move in concert: a condition that increases O2 delivery efficiency (e.g., through improved cardiac output distribution) could reduce VO2p without altering local TSI. Conversely, a shoe that alters loading of a specific muscle group might increase local HHb without materially changing whole-body VO2p. Characterising this dissociation is a primary analytical goal.

5.2 Derived Indicators of Local Oxygen Cost

Beyond mean values, the project derives three composite indicators during the running phase: (1) the oxygenation slope — the rate of HHb increase from run onset, reflecting the speed of local O2 extraction ramp-up; (2) the recovery AUC — the area under the O2Hb re-saturation curve during recovery, reflecting how quickly local oxygenation is restored; and (3) the VO2–mNIRS ratio — the ratio of global VO2p to local HHb amplitude, which provides a preliminary indicator of whether a footwear condition redistributes metabolic load locally or changes systemic efficiency.

5.3 Role of Force Plates

Ground reaction force (GRF) data from the force plates provide the mechanical layer of interpretation. Vertical impulse per stride, contact time, and loading rate are computed for each footwear condition. If carbon-plate shoes reduce GRF variability or alter contact time in a way that reduces muscle co-activation demands, this should manifest as changes in both local mNIRS signals and potentially in VO2p. Integrating force metrics thus helps distinguish energetic from mechanical mechanisms of footwear action.

6. Exploratory Results (Single-Participant Phase)

Anti-hallucination notice: The project is in its single-participant (N = 1) data collection and pipeline validation phase. No cohort-level statistical results are available at the time of writing. The observations reported below are strictly exploratory, apply only to participant Allan LEPLAT, and are presented solely to validate that the pipeline produces physiologically plausible outputs. They cannot support causal inference or generalisation.

Pipeline validation confirms that the four data modalities are successfully ingested from their respective CSV files, synchronised to the COSMED K5 trigger reference, and segmented into the four experimental phases for each footwear condition. Signal continuity checks show that interpolated gaps represent less than 5% of total samples in the NIRS and K5 streams for this participant, which is within acceptable bounds for exploratory analysis. The force-plate channel exhibits the expected high-frequency bilateral alternation pattern consistent with bipedal running at 12.4 km/h, confirming correct time alignment relative to the NIRS signal.

At the descriptive level, the carbon-plate condition (B) is associated with a higher observed VO2p compared to the foam condition (A) for this participant, a direction that may reflect individual gait adaptation to the shoe's mechanical constraints rather than a population-level efficiency advantage. This observation is entirely exploratory and will require cohort-level LMM inference to interpret meaningfully. Local oxygenation profiles across conditions show the expected deoxygenation trajectory during the running phase and resaturation during recovery, confirming that the mNIRS sensors are responding to metabolic demand as expected.

7. Discussion

7.1 Methodological Contributions

The principal methodological contribution of the DEPOXY project at this stage is the design and validation of a reproducible multimodal preprocessing pipeline capable of fusing breath-by-breath metabolic data, continuous NIRS signals, high-frequency ground reaction force data, and heart rate into a single analysis-ready structure. The decision to implement this pipeline in Python — with explicit fallback strategies for trigger reconstruction and conservative gap-flagging for interpolation — reflects a deliberate prioritisation of reproducibility and data integrity over pipeline complexity.

7.2 Inter-Individual Variability

Running economy is one of the most inter-individually variable physiological traits in endurance sport. The planned cohort of ~20 participants will expose the full range of this variability and will determine whether any footwear-related oxygenation effect is consistent across individuals or limited to specific responder profiles. The random-effects structure of the planned LMMs is explicitly designed to accommodate this variability without inflating Type I error.

7.3 Footwear Condition-Dependency

The expectation that carbon-plate footwear uniformly reduces VO2p has been nuanced by recent literature: effects appear to depend on running speed, individual biomechanics, and the specific shoe-runner interaction. The ABCCBA crossover design, combined with the mNIRS layer, provides the methodological resolution to test whether metabolic benefits manifest globally (reduced VO2p), locally (altered TSI/HHb profiles), or as a dissociation between the two — a distinction that pure metabolic analyses cannot address.

7.4 Limitations

Current limitations include the single-participant dataset, which precludes statistical inference; the laboratory treadmill setting, which may not fully replicate overground running biomechanics; and the absence of electromyographic data, which would strengthen interpretation of the mNIRS–force plate relationship. Future work will address cohort expansion, ecological validity testing, and muscle-specific NIRS probe placement optimisation.

8. Conclusion and Perspectives

The DEPOXY project establishes a technically rigorous, reproducible multimodal analysis framework for investigating the metabolic and oxygenation consequences of footwear design during running. The Python preprocessing pipeline successfully resolves the core technical challenge of asynchronous multimodal signal fusion, and the R statistical framework in main.Rmd provides the inferential tools necessary to characterise footwear effects at both global and local physiological levels.

Three key messages emerge at this stage: first, multimodal integration — not any single modality in isolation — is necessary to fully characterise footwear–physiology interactions; second, the VO2p–mNIRS dissociation is a scientifically meaningful signal that carbon-plate technology may selectively engage; and third, the pipeline's modular design means that adding participants, additional NIRS sites, or new footwear conditions requires no structural changes to the analysis workflow, supporting scalability toward the planned full cohort.

Practical Applications

Beyond academic contribution, the DEPOXY framework has direct application in sports engineering (informing midsole and plate design with muscle-specific metabolic feedback), performance monitoring (providing practitioners with objective local oxygenation indicators complementary to existing metabolic tests), and personalised footwear recommendation (identifying responder profiles for carbon-plate versus standard foam technologies).
